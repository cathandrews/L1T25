Propagating Succulents

How to Propagate Succulents from Cuttings and Leaves

Step 1

Succulents kept in small pots require frequent maintenance, particularly if they do not receive enough light. Insufficient sunlight causes the plant to become leggy like this. These plants are perfect for propagation!

Step 2

Although this plant still looks normal from the top, the lower leaves will gradually wither and fall off, leaving a long stem with a rosette high above the soil. Propagation provides an opportunity to make use of these leaves before they start to die.

Step 3

Start by removing the lower leaves. Be careful when you remove the leaves from the stem to ensure a clean break. Hold the leaf gently and wiggle it from side to side until you feel a little snap. If you tear the leaf or leave the base still attached to the stem, it will not be able to grow roots or a new plant.

Step 4

This is what the leaves will look like if they are pulled cleanly from the stem, remaining fully intact.

Step 5

Once you feel like the leaf ends are dry enough, you can place them on top of some well-draining cactus or succulent soil. I like to use a mix of potting soil and perlite. I have had the most successful rooting when I keep my leaves indoors in a window with lots of indirect sunlight.

Step 6

After you have successfully removed the lower leaves you will be left with a small rosette on a long bare stem. I use a pair of craft scissors, but a sharp knife would work great as well. Cut the stem about an inch below the rosette. Make sure your cut is as straight as possible.

Step 7

Now you should have a rosette with a short stem and a long bare stem with no rosette. Don’t throw that away! We’ll use that too. Let the cut ends of your rosette and stem dry out for a few days. This is called callousing over and it helps to prevent rot.

Step 8

Let the cut ends of your rosette and stem dry out for a few days. This is called callousing over and it helps to prevent rot.

Step 9

Once the stem of the rosette has dried out and calloused over, simply place your plant back in a pot with well-draining cactus or succulent soil and it will grow roots again and continue to flourish!

Step 10

Here are some leaves I propagated a few weeks ago. Can you see the little pink roots sprouting from the ends of the leaves? Very soon after seeing roots, you will see teeny tiny baby plants.

Step 11

I water the leaves very rarely until I start to see roots or babies appearing, after which I spray them lightly with water using a spray bottle every few days, allowing the soil to dry between waterings.

Step 12

I let my baby plants grow in rows until I start to notice the “mother leaf” starting to wither and shrivel away. At that point I will carefully remove the leaf and then place the baby plant in its own pot. This can be tricky as you don’t want to accidentally remove the roots with the leaf. You may prefer to play it safe and let the leaf fall off on its own.

Step 13

In only a few weeks your cuttings will grow into full-sized plants! At this point it's a good idea to give the baby plants a good soak about once a week. Too much water is not good, so I recommend wetting the baby plants with a spray bottle once a day instead of completely soaking the soil.

Step 14

Instead of using individual pots with this batch, I just removed the leaves as they withered and left the plants to grow together like a little succulent forest.

Step 15

Back to that stump. No part of this plant is going to go to waste!

Simply put this pot aside and eventually it will begin to sprout new plants from each place we removed a leaf!

Step 16

Here is another example of a leggy succulent that was propagated. You can see the new growth at the base of the plant where the old leaves were removed.
